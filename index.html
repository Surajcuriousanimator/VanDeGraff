01 · The Inventor

The man who carried charge
on a silk ribbon

Van de Graaff began as a power-company engineer in Alabama. In Paris he heard Marie Curie lecture on radioactivity, and at Oxford he absorbed the great problem of the age: to probe the nucleus, physicists needed particles accelerated through millions of steady volts — and no machine on Earth could supply them.

His answer was disarmingly mechanical: don't push charge through a wire against a potential — carry it there on an insulating belt. Deposit the charge inside a hollow sphere, where the field is zero, and the sphere will swallow it without complaint, forever. Voltage limited only by the air itself.

1901 · TUSCALOOSA

An engineer first

Born in Alabama; studies engineering and works a year for Alabama Power before physics claims him in Paris and Oxford.

1929 · PRINCETON

The first generator — 80,000 volts

Built from a tin can, a silk ribbon and a small motor. Crude, but the principle — mechanical transport of charge into a hollow conductor — works perfectly on the first try.

1931 · NEW YORK

1.5 million volts, demonstrated live

A polished double-sphere machine stuns the inaugural dinner of the American Institute of Physics. The press hails "man-made lightning".

1933 · ROUND HILL, MIT

The colossus

Twin 12-metre columns crowned by 4.6-metre aluminium spheres reach ~5 MV. Today it towers inside the Museum of Science, Boston — still the largest air-insulated Van de Graaff in the world.

1937 → TODAY

The accelerator age

Pressurised Van de Graaff and tandem accelerators drive nuclear physics, materials analysis, carbon dating and cancer therapy. The classroom dome that raises your hair is the same physics, scaled down.

Figure 1.63 · Van de Graaff generator

PRINCIPLE

  • Works on the principle of electrostatic induction and action at points (corona discharge).
  • Designed by Robert Van de Graaff in 1929; produces a large electrostatic potential difference, up to several million volts (10⁷ V).

CONSTRUCTION

  1. A large hollow spherical conductor A is fixed on an insulating stand.
  2. A pulley B is mounted at the centre of the hollow sphere, and another pulley C is fixed at the bottom.
  3. A belt of insulating material (silk or rubber) runs over both pulleys; pulley C is driven continuously by an electric motor.
  4. Two comb-shaped metallic conductors D and E are fixed near the pulleys: comb D near pulley C is maintained at a positive potential of 10⁴ V by a power supply; comb E near pulley B is connected to the inner side of the hollow sphere.

WORKING

  1. Due to the high electric field near comb D, the air between the belt and comb D gets ionised. Positive charges are pushed towards the belt and stick to it; negative charges are attracted towards comb D.
  2. The belt carries the positive charges upward.
  3. When they reach comb E, a large amount of negative and positive charge is induced on either side of comb E by electrostatic induction. The positive charges are pushed away from comb E and reach the outer surface of the sphere, where they distribute uniformly (the sphere is a conductor).
  4. Simultaneously, the negative charges on comb E nullify the positive charges on the belt through corona discharge before the belt passes over the pulley — so the descending belt carries almost no net charge.
  5. At the bottom the belt is charged again, and the cycle repeats. Charge accumulates until the potential reaches the limiting value ≈ 10⁷ V.
  6. Beyond this limit, charge leaks to the surroundings because the surrounding air gets ionised. Leakage is reduced by enclosing the machine in a gas-filled steel chamber at very high pressure.

APPLICATION

  • The high voltage is used to accelerate positive ions (protons, deuterons) for nuclear disintegration experiments and other applications.

1

Ionise — action at points

Comb D's sharp points concentrate the field until air molecules are torn into ions. Positive ions are repelled onto the belt; electrons rush into the comb. Watch the golden flashes.

2

Transport — work against the field

The motor hauls the charged belt upward against the electric repulsion of the dome's charge. Every centimetre of lift converts mechanical work into electrostatic potential energy.

3

Induce & collect

The positive belt induces − on comb E's points and + on the dome. The points glow with corona, spraying negative charge that neutralises the belt — net effect: the dome inherits the charge.

4

Accumulate — Gauss's gift

Charge spreads uniformly over the outer surface; the field inside stays exactly zero. The incoming belt never feels the megavolts already stored. Potential climbs relentlessly.

5

Saturate — the air surrenders

Near 10⁷ V the surface field exceeds air's dielectric strength. Corona haze and streamers bleed charge away as fast as the belt delivers it: the limiting value. Cure: a pressurised steel chamber.

05 · Graduate-Level Physics

Why it works — with the
mathematics that proves it

🧿

Gauss's law: the dome never fills up

Inside a conductor in electrostatic equilibrium, E = 0. A Gaussian surface just inside the shell encloses zero net field — so any charge handed to the inner wall is driven entirely to the outer surface. The interior remains at the dome's potential but field-free: comb E always works in calm air, no matter how monstrous the stored charge.

∮ E · dA = qencε₀ ⇒ Einside = 0
🌐

Potential of the sphere

A charged sphere of radius R behaves, outside, like a point charge at its centre. Its potential and surface field are locked together — and the ratio is simply R. This single line explains why bigger domes hold more volts.

V = 14πε₀QR , Esurface = VR
📍

Action at points — corona discharge

For conductors in contact, charge arranges so that σ ∝ 1/r: the smaller the radius of curvature, the denser the charge and the fiercer the field. At a comb tip (r ~ 10 μm), a mere 10⁴ V produces fields far beyond breakdown — a private, controlled lightning storm that sprays charge across the air gap. Both combs run on it.

Etip ≈ Vr ≫ 3×10⁶ V/m
💥

The ceiling: dielectric breakdown

Dry air insulates only below Eb ≈ 3×10⁶ V/m. Since Esurface = V/R, the dome saturates at Vmax = EbR. A 3.3 m sphere ⇒ 10⁷ V — exactly your textbook's limiting value. Pressurised gas (10–20 atm N₂/SF₆) multiplies Eb, which is how accelerator machines beat the limit.

Vmax = Eb · R
🔁

The belt is a current source

A belt of width w moving at speed v, carrying surface charge density σ, delivers a steady current — the machine is physically a constant-current generator charging the capacitance C = 4πε₀R of its own dome. σ itself is capped by breakdown at the belt surface: σmax = 2ε₀Eb ≈ 53 μC/m².

I = σwv , dVdt = IC
⚖️

Equilibrium: a differential equation

The dome's voltage obeys C·dV/dt = Ibelt − Ileak(V). Leakage (corona + load) grows steeply with V, so the system self-regulates to the voltage where delivery exactly balances loss — which is why a real machine's voltage is rock-steady. That stability is precisely what made it the perfect particle accelerator.

CdVdt = Ibelt − Ileak(V) = 0 at Veq
Stored energy: U = ½CV². A 1 m dome at 3 MV stores ~½ kJ — released in a microsecond spark, that's a peak power of hundreds of megawatts. Hence the crack.

Model answers, ready to write

Board examiners award marks for specific phrases and values. These model answers contain every one of them. Open, read aloud twice, reproduce with the diagram.

2 MARKS State the principle of a Van de Graaff generator. ▾

The Van de Graaff generator works on the principle of electrostatic induction and action at points (corona discharge). It produces a large electrostatic potential difference of the order of 10⁷ V.

3 MARKS Why does charge accumulate only on the outer surface of the sphere? ▾
  1. The dome is a conductor; in electrostatic equilibrium the field inside a conductor is zero.
  2. By Gauss's law, a surface drawn just inside the shell encloses zero net charge — so all excess charge must reside on the outer surface, distributed uniformly.
  3. Hence charge delivered to the inside by comb E feels no repulsion from charge already stored, and the dome can be charged continuously to very high potential.
5 MARKS Describe the construction and working of a Van de Graaff generator with a neat diagram. ▾

Principle: electrostatic induction and action at points.

Construction: A large hollow spherical conductor A is fixed on an insulating stand. A pulley B is mounted at the centre of the sphere and a pulley C at the bottom. A belt of insulating material (silk or rubber) runs over the pulleys; pulley C is driven by an electric motor. Comb-shaped conductors D and E are fixed near the pulleys — D is maintained at +10⁴ V by a power supply, E is connected to the inner side of the sphere. (Draw Figure 1.63 with all labels.)

Working: The strong field at comb D ionises the air; positive charges are sprayed onto the belt and carried upward. Near comb E, electrostatic induction induces negative charge on the comb (and positive charge on the sphere); the positive charges are pushed to the outer surface of the sphere, where they spread uniformly. The negative charges of comb E neutralise the belt by corona discharge, so the descending belt is almost uncharged. The process repeats, and the potential rises to the limiting value of about 10⁷ V. Beyond this, charge leaks to the surroundings by ionisation of air; leakage is minimised by enclosing the machine in a gas-filled steel chamber at very high pressure.

Use: to accelerate positive ions (protons, deuterons) for nuclear disintegration experiments.

2 MARKS Why can't the potential rise beyond 10⁷ V, and how is leakage reduced? ▾

Beyond ~10⁷ V the electric field near the dome's surface exceeds the dielectric strength of air (≈ 3×10⁶ V/m). The surrounding air gets ionised, and the extra charge leaks away — this sets the limiting value. Leakage is reduced by enclosing the entire machine in a gas-filled steel chamber at very high pressure.

🚫 The four marks students lose — and how not to

  • Writing "electromagnetic induction". It is electrostatic induction — there are no changing magnetic fields anywhere in this machine.
  • Swapping the combs. D is at the bottom (+10⁴ V, sprays); E is at the top (connected to the inner surface, collects).
  • Forgetting the two numbers. 10⁴ V at comb D, 10⁷ V limiting potential. Examiners scan for both.
  • Saying charge sits "on the dome". Say "uniformly on the outer surface of the hollow sphere" — the word outer carries the mark.
